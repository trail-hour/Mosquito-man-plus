Is West Nile Virus New to Durham & the GTA?

If you've seen a headline pairing West Nile virus with a Durham Region or GTA city this week, the virus itself isn't new. Public health units test mosquito pools for West Nile virus every summer, and a positive result is a normal part of that surveillance, not evidence of a new outbreak. What's new is the 2026 season's specific results, which we're tracking region by region below as local health units confirm them.

The overall risk to any one person stays low. An estimated 70-80% of people bitten by an infected mosquito never develop symptoms at all, and only a small share of cases become severe. Still, a confirmed positive is useful information: it tells homeowners where mosquito pressure is building and when it's worth tightening up prevention, or booking a barrier spray to knock down the resting mosquito population around a property before pressure builds further.

Durham Region (Ajax): First Positive Batch of the Season

Durham Region confirmed its first West Nile virus-positive mosquito batch of the 2026 season on July 17, from a trap in Ajax. That timing is consistent with previous years, when Durham's first positive result typically lands sometime in mid-to-late July as mosquito populations peak through the hottest, most humid stretch of summer.

A positive trap result doesn't mean every mosquito in the area is carrying the virus, but it is the point in the season where prevention starts mattering more. That applies across the rest of Durham Region too, including Oshawa, Whitby, Pickering, and Clarington, since mosquitoes move freely between neighbouring municipalities and a positive result in one trap usually signals similar pressure nearby. You can follow official updates on Durham Region's West Nile virus page.

Toronto (Scarborough & North York): First Positive Confirmed

Toronto Public Health confirmed its first West Nile virus-positive mosquitoes of 2026 on July 17, matching Durham Region's timing almost exactly. For properties in Scarborough and North York, this is the same seasonal surveillance process running as expected, and it lines up with when Toronto typically records its first positive result most summers. See Toronto Public Health's announcement for the full details.

York Region (Vaughan): First Positive Found in Markham Traps

York Region's first West Nile-positive mosquitoes of the season turned up in two Markham traps around July 16, 2026. The positive traps themselves were in Markham, but York Region's mosquito surveillance and prevention guidance applies across the region, including Vaughan, where the same seasonal pressure and timing apply. CBC's coverage has the details on both York and Halton's results this year.

Halton: The Earliest GTA Positive This Year

Halton actually recorded the earliest confirmed West Nile-positive result in the GTA this year, on July 2, 2026, weeks ahead of Durham, Toronto, and York. That earlier timing is a useful reminder that the exact start of West Nile activity varies by region and by year, driven mostly by local weather and standing water conditions. Halton isn't part of our direct service area, but its early result is part of the same regional picture GTA homeowners should keep an eye on. See the same CBC report for Halton's numbers.

Peel Region (Mississauga): Season Advisory, No Confirmed Case Yet

As of mid-July 2026, Peel Region hasn't confirmed a West Nile-positive mosquito batch yet this season, and current guidance is a general seasonal advisory rather than a specific trap result. Based on last year's timeline, when Peel's first 2025 positive was confirmed around July 25, a first 2026 result is a reasonably likely next step in the coming weeks.

Homeowners in Mississauga shouldn't wait for an official positive before taking prevention seriously, since mosquito populations are already active regardless of when a lab result comes back. Peel's City of Mississauga seasonal update has the latest advisory details.

Reducing Your Risk: Prevention That Actually Works

Regardless of which region you're in, the same basic steps cut mosquito exposure the most: empty or remove standing water around the property at least once a week, since that's where mosquitoes lay eggs; use a Health Canada-approved insect repellent when spending time outdoors, especially at dusk and dawn when mosquitoes are most active; cover up with long sleeves and pants during those peak hours if you're outside for a while; and keep window and door screens in good repair so mosquitoes stay outside.

Those habits reduce personal exposure, but they don't do much about the resting mosquito population already established around a yard, especially near shaded areas, tall grass, or standing water sources that aren't easy to eliminate completely. That's where a professional barrier spray makes the biggest difference, targeting the adult mosquitoes resting in vegetation around a property rather than just reacting to bites as they happen. Frequently Asked Questions

Is West Nile virus new to Durham Region and the GTA?

No. Public health units test mosquito pools for West Nile virus every summer, and finding a positive result is routine seasonal surveillance, not a new outbreak. What changes year to year is the timing and location of each season's first positive results.

What is the actual risk of getting West Nile virus?

Overall risk is low. An estimated 70-80% of people bitten by an infected mosquito develop no symptoms at all, and only a small share of cases become severe.

Which parts of Durham Region and the GTA have confirmed West Nile-positive mosquitoes in 2026?

As of mid-July 2026, positive results have been confirmed in Ajax (Durham Region), Scarborough and North York (Toronto), Markham (York Region), and Halton. Peel Region (Mississauga) is under a season advisory with no confirmed positive yet.

How can I protect my property from mosquitoes carrying West Nile virus?

Remove standing water weekly, use a Health Canada-approved repellent outdoors, cover up during dusk and dawn when mosquitoes are most active, keep window and door screens in good repair, and consider a professional barrier spray to reduce the resting mosquito population around your yard.

When will this tracker be updated?

This page is reviewed and updated as public health units confirm new positive results, so check back for the latest regional status rather than looking for a separate new post.
