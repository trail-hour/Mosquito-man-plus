What Actually Draws Mosquitoes to a Property

Mosquitoes are drawn to a property for two separate reasons: somewhere to breed, and somewhere to rest and feed. Standing water covers the first, since female mosquitoes need it to lay eggs, and it doesn't take much. A few centimetres in a clogged eavestrough, a forgotten plant saucer, or a low spot in the lawn after rain is enough. Shaded, humid areas cover the second, giving adult mosquitoes a place to hide from heat and predators between feedings. A Durham Region yard with both, water and shade, is a far bigger draw than one with just one or neither.

Body Heat, CO2, and Scent: Why Some People Attract More Mosquitoes

It's not just about the yard. Female mosquitoes locate hosts using a combination of body heat, carbon dioxide from breathing, and scent cues from skin bacteria, and people naturally vary in all three. Larger body size, higher metabolic rate, and certain skin microbiomes can all make someone a more attractive target, regardless of what they're wearing or where they're standing at a backyard gathering. This is why mosquito pressure can feel uneven across a group of people at the same event in Oshawa or Whitby, with some guests barely noticing them while others are swatting all evening. Even a well-treated yard won't erase that personal variation entirely, but it dramatically reduces how many mosquitoes are around to act on it.

Yard Features That Make a Property More Attractive

  • Standing water — gutters, birdbaths, plant saucers, kiddie pools, and low-lying lawn sections
  • Dense shrubs and tall grass — shaded resting spots for adult mosquitoes during the day
  • Ponds and water features — especially still water without a pump or fountain to keep it moving
  • Proximity to ravines, creeks, or wooded lots — common in parts of Oshawa, Clarington, and Bowmanville, adding outside breeding pressure regardless of yard maintenance
  • Certain outdoor lighting — some light types draw mosquitoes closer to doors and patios at dusk

Reducing What Attracts Mosquitoes in the First Place

Walk the property after every rainfall and empty anything holding water. Keep gutters clear, since clogged sections are one of the most commonly overlooked breeding sites. Trim back dense shrubs and tall grass, particularly along fence lines and shaded corners. If there's a pond or water feature, keep it moving with a small pump or fountain, since mosquitoes prefer still water for laying eggs. Moving bright lights farther from doors and seating areas also helps keep evening activity from concentrating right where people gather.

These habits meaningfully cut down what's attracting mosquitoes in the first place, but they won't fully offset a property with heavy pressure from nearby wooded areas or ravines. In those cases, our mosquito control services target the resting sites and breeding water that yard maintenance alone can't fully reach. Frequently Asked Questions

What yard feature attracts the most mosquitoes?

Standing water is the single biggest draw, since mosquitoes need it to lay eggs. Even a small amount in a clogged gutter, plant saucer, or forgotten container is enough to support breeding.

Why do mosquitoes bite some people more than others?

Female mosquitoes locate hosts using body heat, carbon dioxide, and scent cues from skin bacteria, and these vary from person to person. It's not about where someone stands or what they wear.

Does grass length affect mosquito activity?

Yes. Tall grass and dense, unkempt shrubs give adult mosquitoes shaded places to rest during the day, so keeping grass trimmed and shrubs thinned reduces the resting habitat available on a property.

Do lights attract mosquitoes?

Mosquitoes are drawn to certain types of outdoor lighting, especially at dusk. Keeping bright lights farther from doors and seating areas, or switching to yellow/warm-toned bulbs, can reduce how many gather near the house in the evening.

Can I fully eliminate what attracts mosquitoes to my yard?

You can significantly reduce it through consistent water removal and yard maintenance, but properties near ravines, creeks, or wooded areas will still see pressure from breeding grounds just outside the property line, which is where professional treatment tends to make the biggest difference.
